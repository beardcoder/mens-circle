## Refresh vs Rebrand

| | Refresh | Rebrand |
|-|---------|---------|
| **Scope** | Modernize existing elements | Fundamental identity change |
| **Triggers** | Dated visuals, new medium requirements | New positioning, merger, reputation reset |
| **Timeline** | Weeks to months | Months to years |
| **Risk** | Low — gradual, recognizable | High — can alienate existing audience |
| **Brand equity** | Preserved | Partially sacrificed |

Most brands need refreshes, not rebrands. A rebrand should be the last resort.

## Signs a Brand Needs Updating

1. **Visual fatigue** — The style looks dated compared to industry peers
2. **Medium mismatch** — The brand was designed for print and doesn't work digitally
3. **Audience shift** — The target audience has changed but the brand hasn't
4. **Consistency collapse** — Different teams have drifted into different visual languages
5. **Negative associations** — The brand carries baggage from past incidents
6. **Growth beyond origin** — The brand has outgrown its original positioning

## Evolution Strategies

### Evolutionary (Low Risk)

Change one element at a time:
1. Update the color palette (modernize tones)
2. Swap typography (fresher fonts, same personality)
3. Refine the logo (simplify, not replace)
4. Update imagery style (photography direction, illustration)

### Seasonal/Campaign Variations

Temporary brand expressions for campaigns, seasons, or events:
- Modified color palette (holiday accents)
- Special edition imagery
- Campaign-specific messaging
- **Always return to core** after the campaign

## Maintaining Equity

During any brand change:

1. **Keep the most recognizable element** — Usually the logo symbol or primary color
2. **Communicate the change** — Explain why, especially for B2B
3. **Phase the rollout** — Don't change everything at once
4. **Measure recognition** — Track whether existing customers still recognize you
5. **Update all touchpoints** — Half-updated brands look broken, not evolving
